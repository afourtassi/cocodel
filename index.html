ABOUT OUR RESEARCH

Children learn from direct observation and exploration, but much of the knowledge they acquire comes from the language they hear around them. Understanding how linguistic communication scaffolds children’s linguistic and conceptual development is, thus, one of the most exciting frontiers in psychology, with important implications for education (e.g., improving science teaching practices), health (e.g., mitigating learning problems in children with communicative difficulties), and artificial intelligence (e.g., designing virtual learning companions for children).
Our group investigates this scientific question using an interdisciplinary and integrative approach

Naturalistic

We study children's communication and development in the natural context (e.g., at home or in the classroom)

Computational

We use computational methods (e.g., NLP, Network science, and Bayesian cognitive models) to build quantitative theories

Experimental

We test specific hypotheses about the role of communication in knowledge acquisition using fun laboratory studies

Practical

We collaborate with experts in applied research both to enrich our theories and to develop theory-informed applications

Children learn from direct observation and exploration (Piaget, 1954), but much of the knowledge they acquire comes from the language they hear around them (Vygostsky, 1978; Gelman, 2009; Harris et al., 2018). Understanding how linguistic communication scaffolds children's linguistic and conceptual development is, thus, one of the most exciting frontiers in psychology, with important implications for research in education, social impairment, and artificial intelligence.

Advances in the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP) have allowed researchers to investigate this phenomenon in its natural environment, going beyond simplified laboratory contexts (Dupoux, 2018). In particular, methods in conversational systems have improved dramatically in recent years, leading to important industrial applications (e.g., chatbots and personal assistants). In this project, we use these methods as scientific tools to 1) study the mechanisms of communicative coordination (e.g., Clark and Brennen, 1991) and argumentation (e.g., Toulmin, 1958) between children and their caregivers in natural conversations, and 2) to explore how these mechanisms mediate knowledge acquisition and conceptual change in children.

We will capitalize on existing data from CHILDES (MacWhinney, 2000) — the largest corpus of child-caregiver spontaneous conversations. We will also collect new developmental data, observing child-caregiver conversational dynamics in two designs involving: a) communicative coordination towards achieving a common goal, and b) communicative argumentation about children's naive conceptions. Both designs lead to quantifiable learning outcomes, allowing us to observe how conversational strategies influence knowledge transmission.

The project is led by the Computational Communication & Development team (CoCoDev), an interdisciplinary research group within the Institute of Language, Communication, and the Brain at Aix-Marseille University.
